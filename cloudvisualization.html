Low-Level Clouds
Surface to 6,500 ft AGL
Composition & behavior
Mostly made of water droplets, sometimes supercooled. These clouds can cause icing — bad news for pilots. Low clouds reduce visibility, create low ceilings, and shift rapidly, often grounding VFR flights.
Examples
Stratus, Stratocumulus, Nimbostratus
Impact on operations
Poor visibility, potential icing, low ceilings
Mid-Level Clouds
6,500 to 20,000 ft AGL
Composition & behavior
Formed from water droplets, ice crystals, or both — including supercooled water. These clouds can signal shifting weather patterns and moderate turbulence.
Examples
Altostratus, Altocumulus
Impact on operations
Light icing and turbulence, weather transitions
High-Level Clouds
Above 20,000 ft AGL
Composition & behavior
Formed in stable air, composed of ice crystals. Typically bring calm skies and pose little threat.
Examples
Cirrus, Cirrostratus, Cirrocumulus
Impact on operations
No turbulence or icing — great for scenic flights
Clouds With Vertical Development
Low/Mid-level base · Extends to high altitudes
Behavior
Starting from low or mid-levels and rising sky-high. They build upward due to atmospheric instability — the higher they go, the more dramatic the weather.
Towering Cumulus
Early signs of turbulence and instability
Cumulonimbus
The powerhouses of hazardous weather — thunderstorms, lightning, hail, tornadoes, wind shear, and severe turbulence
